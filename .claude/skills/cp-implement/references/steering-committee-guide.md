# Steering Committee Guide — Implementation Phase

> Reference for cp:implement Activity 3. Covers agenda structure, decision documentation, escalation protocols, and managing difficult governance conversations.

## Purpose of the Steering Committee

The steering committee has three roles during implementation:

1. **Decision authority:** Approve choices that are above workstream owner authority (budget, scope, personnel)
2. **Blocker removal:** Remove senior-level obstacles that workstream teams cannot resolve themselves
3. **Strategic guidance:** Ensure implementation stays aligned with the original recommendation

**What the steering committee is NOT:**
- A status reporting forum (status goes to sponsor in writing; steering committee time is for decisions)
- A place to litigate the recommendation (the recommendation was approved; the steering committee oversees execution)
- A place for the consulting team to demonstrate how much work they've done

---

## Steering Committee Cadence and Format

**Frequency:** Monthly during active implementation; quarterly during sustainability phase.

**Duration:** 60-90 minutes maximum.

**Participants:**

| Role | Function | Mandatory? |
|------|---------|-----------|
| Client sponsor | Decision authority; chairs the meeting | Always |
| Workstream owners (client) | Status update owners; decision input | Always |
| Lead consultant | Facilitates; presents recommendations | Always |
| Client project manager | Logistics; decision log | Always |
| Other senior stakeholders | Input on specific decisions | As needed |

**Pre-meeting preparation:**

1. **Pre-read (T-48h):** Steering committee deck sent to all participants 48 hours before the meeting
2. **Pre-read expectations:** Participants are expected to read the deck; the meeting is for decisions, not for reading slides together
3. **Decision pre-positioning:** Lead consultant calls or emails key stakeholders before the meeting to preview difficult decisions and understand their positions

## Steering Committee Deck Structure

### Section 1: Summary (1 slide) — First 5 minutes

**Headline:** "[Engagement] is [Green/Amber/Red] — [N] decisions needed today"

Content:
- Overall RAG status with trend (improving / stable / declining)
- Key milestone achieved since last meeting
- Key milestone coming in next period
- Number of decisions needed today (list them)

### Section 2: Workstream Status (1-2 slides) — Next 10 minutes

**Headline:** "[N] workstreams Green, [N] Amber, [N] Red"

Content:
- RAG by workstream (traffic light table)
- Milestones achieved vs. planned
- Key milestones next period
- Note: workstream owners present their own RAG with a 60-second verbal update — not a slide

### Section 3: Impact Tracking (1 slide) — Next 10 minutes

**Headline:** "Impact tracking [on track / ahead / behind] — [$ or %] realized to date vs [target]"

Content:
- Impact metrics: baseline / target / current / % of target
- Trend chart (if available)
- Commentary on trajectory

### Section 4: Decisions Needed (1 slide per decision) — Core of the meeting

Each decision gets its own slide:

```
DECISION SLIDE STRUCTURE:

Title: "Decision needed: [Specific decision]"

Context (2-3 bullets): Why this decision is needed now
Options: 
  Option A: [Description] → Impact: [+/-] | Risk: [H/M/L]
  Option B: [Description] → Impact: [+/-] | Risk: [H/M/L]
  Option C: [Description] → Impact: [+/-] | Risk: [H/M/L]

Recommendation: Option [X] — because [1-2 sentence rationale]

Decision: [Leave blank — to be filled in the meeting]
Owner: [Who implements if approved]
Deadline: [When]
```

### Section 5: Risks (1 slide) — 5 minutes

**Headline:** "Top [N] risks this period; [N] new risks since last meeting"

Content:
- Top 3-5 risks with probability, impact, and mitigation status
- Flag any risks that have moved from Amber to Red since last meeting
- Ask: any risks the steering committee is aware of that the team has missed?

### Section 6: Next Period (1 slide) — 5 minutes

**Headline:** "Key actions next 30 days"

Content:
- 3-5 key milestones next period
- Decisions expected at next steering committee
- Any inputs or access needed from the steering committee before next meeting

## Running the Decision Conversation

The most critical skill in steering committee facilitation is running decision conversations effectively.

### Decision conversation protocol

**Step 1 — Frame clearly:** "We need to decide X. Here are the three options. Our recommendation is Y because Z. Does anyone have questions on the options before we discuss?"

**Step 2 — Hear input:** Let participants speak. Don't defend the recommendation immediately. Note objections.

**Step 3 — Address objections:** "I hear the concern about [X]. Here's how that factors into our recommendation: [response]."

**Step 4 — Call the question:** "Based on this discussion, I'd like to call for a decision. Is there any objection to moving forward with Option Y?"

**Step 5 — Document:** Write the decision in the decision log while in the room. Read it back: "To confirm, the decision is [X], owned by [Name], to be completed by [Date]."

### When the decision doesn't get made

Sometimes the steering committee cannot make a decision in the meeting:

| Reason | Appropriate response |
|--------|---------------------|
| Missing information | "We'll gather [specific data] and bring this decision to a special session within 10 days" |
| Disagreement between senior stakeholders | Sponsor makes the call; or schedule 1:1 between disagreeing parties before the next session |
| Scope of decision exceeds steering committee authority | Escalate to the appropriate level (board, CEO); document the escalation |
| Sponsor defers | "We need a decision by [date] for [reason]. If we don't decide by then, we'll default to [Option X]." |

**Never leave a decision undecided without a clear next step and deadline.**

## Managing Difficult Steering Committee Conversations

### When a workstream is significantly behind

1. Don't surprise the room — warn the sponsor in advance of the meeting
2. Come with a recovery plan (3 options) — not just a problem statement
3. Frame as a "course correction needed" not a "failure"
4. Ask the steering committee for a specific decision or resource, not just acknowledgment

### When a senior stakeholder challenges the recommendation

1. Acknowledge the concern: "That's an important point. Can you tell me more about what's driving it?"
2. Stay calm and curious — don't be defensive
3. Distinguish between: factual disagreement (resolvable with data) / value disagreement (requires negotiation) / political disagreement (requires sponsor intervention)
4. If the challenge has merit: "We'll investigate and report back at the next session" — don't capitulate in the room without analysis
5. If the challenge doesn't have merit: "We considered [alternative] and here's why we recommended against it: [evidence]"

### When the sponsor is disengaged

Signs: arriving late, phone in hand, delegating decisions to the room without engaging.

Response:
1. Pre-call before the next meeting: "I want to make sure we use your time well. Here are the 2 decisions I need from you specifically."
2. Shorten future meetings: if the meeting can't hold sponsor attention, it's too long
3. Use asynchronous decisions: some decisions can be made via email with a 48h window; save the meeting for decisions that need real-time discussion
