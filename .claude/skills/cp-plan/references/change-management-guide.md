# Change Management Guide — Consulting Engagements

> Reference for cp:plan Activity 5. Use to design the change management approach for the Implementation Plan.

## Why Change Management Matters

McKinsey research (and subsequent work by Kotter, Prosci, and others) consistently finds that 70% of transformation programs fail to achieve their intended goals. The primary cause is not strategy — it is people. Change management is the discipline that addresses the human dimension of implementation.

**The three failure modes:**
1. **Awareness failure:** People don't know why things are changing or what they're changing to
2. **Will failure:** People understand but don't want to change (resistance, inertia, fear)
3. **Skill failure:** People want to change but don't know how (insufficient training, tools, support)

---

## The ADKAR Model

ADKAR (Prosci) is the most widely used individual change model in consulting engagements. It defines five building blocks that every affected person must pass through for change to stick.

| Stage | What it means | How to build it |
|-------|--------------|----------------|
| **Awareness** | I know WHY this is changing | Communications explaining the business rationale; avoid jargon |
| **Desire** | I WANT to support the change | Addressing concerns; involving people in design; demonstrating "what's in it for me" |
| **Knowledge** | I know HOW to work in the new way | Training programs; process documentation; job aids |
| **Ability** | I CAN do it in practice | Practice, coaching, feedback loops; system usability |
| **Reinforcement** | I will KEEP doing it | Performance management aligned to new behavior; recognition; governance |

**ADKAR progression:** Each stage must be built before the next one can work. Training (Knowledge) delivered before Desire is established produces low adoption. Reinforcement without Ability produces frustration.

## Change Management Planning by Phase

### Phase 1 — Foundation (Months 1-3): Build Awareness and Desire

**Objectives:**
- Leadership is visibly sponsoring the change
- All affected staff know what is changing, why, and what it means for them
- Concerns are surfaced and addressed proactively

**Key activities:**

| Activity | Format | Audience | Timing |
|----------|--------|---------|--------|
| Executive communication | Email + all-hands | All staff | Week 1 |
| Leadership cascade | Manager briefings | Managers first | Week 2 |
| Roadshow (for large implementations) | Town halls by location | All affected staff | Weeks 3-4 |
| Open Q&A sessions | Small group (15-20 people) | Self-selected | Weeks 3-6 |
| Concerns register | Anonymous survey | All staff | Weeks 2-3 |

**Concerns register:** Collect concerns anonymously in the first 30 days. Categorize and address in subsequent communications. Showing that concerns were heard (even if not all acted upon) builds trust.

### Phase 2 — Execution (Months 4-9): Build Knowledge and Ability

**Objectives:**
- All staff who need to change how they work are trained
- Practice environments (pilots, simulations) are available
- Coaching is available for people who struggle after training

**Training design principles:**

| Principle | Application |
|-----------|------------|
| Role-specific | Don't train everyone on everything; train each role on what their job requires |
| Learning by doing | Scenarios and practice exercises; not just conceptual explanation |
| Accessible format | Consider different learning styles and schedules (in-person, e-learning, job aids) |
| Immediate application | Training timed to when people will use the new skill (not 3 months early) |
| Reinforced | Brief refreshers 30 and 60 days after initial training |

**Adoption measurement during Phase 2:**

- Training completion rate (target: 100% before go-live)
- Self-assessed confidence survey (before and after training)
- Error rate in new process (if measurable)
- Supervisor-assessed adoption rate

### Phase 3 — Sustainability (Months 10-18): Build Reinforcement

**Objectives:**
- New behaviors are embedded in performance management
- Leadership visibly reinforces the new way of working
- Backsliding is identified and addressed early

**Reinforcement mechanisms:**

| Mechanism | Description | When |
|----------|------------|------|
| Performance metrics | New KPIs tied to new behaviors in performance reviews | Month 12 |
| Recognition | Leadership publicly recognizes teams that adopted well | Throughout |
| Process audits | Random checks to confirm adherence; not punitive | Monthly in Phase 3 |
| Peer champions | Identify early adopters to coach colleagues | From Month 6 |
| Manager accountability | Managers held accountable for their team's adoption rate | From Month 9 |

## Managing Resistance

Resistance is natural. Classifying resistance helps address it correctly.

### Types of resistance

| Type | Description | Signals | Response |
|------|------------|---------|---------|
| **Rational** | Person has a legitimate concern based on facts | Specific objections; questions about feasibility | Listen; address the specific concern; adjust if the concern is valid |
| **Emotional** | Fear, anxiety, uncertainty about personal impact | Disengagement; "this won't work"; excessive cynicism | One-on-one conversation; acknowledge feelings; share specific evidence |
| **Political** | Person perceives the change as a threat to their power or position | Blocking in meetings; mobilizing others against the change | Engage privately; find a role for them in the change; escalate to sponsor if obstructive |
| **Habitual** | Comfort with existing ways; not active resistance but inertia | Missing deadlines; reverting to old process; "too busy" | Structured coaching; clear deadlines; manager accountability |

### The resistor conversation

For key resistors (especially those with formal or informal power):

1. **Acknowledge:** "I understand you have concerns about [X]. I'd like to understand them better."
2. **Listen:** Let them speak for 80% of the conversation. Don't defend.
3. **Reflect:** "What I'm hearing is [their concern]. Is that right?"
4. **Address or escalate:** If the concern is valid → adjust the plan and report back. If the concern is based on a misunderstanding → clarify with evidence. If the concern is political → escalate to sponsor.
5. **Follow up:** Report back within 5 business days on what was done.

## Sponsor Engagement — The Critical Role

Research consistently shows that active, visible sponsor engagement is the #1 predictor of change success. A sponsor who only "approves" in meetings is insufficient.

**What active sponsorship looks like:**

| Active sponsor | Passive sponsor |
|---------------|----------------|
| Mentions the change regularly in team communications | Approves the plan and steps back |
| Addresses resistors directly and promptly | Avoids conflict with resistors |
| Visibly uses new processes themselves | Exempts themselves from new protocols |
| Celebrates early wins publicly | Ignores progress updates |
| Makes decisions when workstreams are blocked | Defers decisions indefinitely |

**Sponsor coaching:** Part of the consulting role in cp:plan and cp:implement is coaching the sponsor to be an active change leader, not a passive approver. This is done 1:1, not in steering committee.
