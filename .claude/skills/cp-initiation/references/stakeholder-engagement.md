# Stakeholder Engagement Guide — cp:initiation

> Reference for Activity 3 of cp:initiation. Use to build the stakeholder map and plan engagement strategies.

## Power/Interest Matrix — Detailed Protocol

### Step 1: Identify all stakeholders

Cast a wide net first — include anyone who:
- Has decision authority over the engagement outcomes
- Controls resources (budget, data, access, people) the engagement needs
- Will be affected by the recommendations
- Has political influence over whether recommendations get implemented

**Sources to mine:**
- Client org chart
- Project sponsor ("who else should I talk to?")
- Press releases, annual reports (external stakeholders)
- Prior engagement files (if re-entering a client)

### Step 2: Score each stakeholder

**Power score (1-5):**
- 5 — Can approve or kill the engagement / recommendations unilaterally
- 4 — Senior influencer; strong voice in final decision
- 3 — Significant department/function authority; can create friction
- 2 — Limited authority; affects implementation more than decisions
- 1 — Peripheral; minimal formal power

**Interest score (1-5):**
- 5 — Deeply invested; outcome directly affects their role, budget, or team
- 4 — Strong interest; monitors progress closely
- 3 — Moderate interest; engaged when directly asked
- 2 — Mild interest; aware but not tracking
- 1 — Low interest; only peripherally aware

### Step 3: Map to quadrant and assign strategy

| Quadrant | Power | Interest | Strategy |
|----------|-------|----------|---------|
| **Manage closely** | High | High | Weekly touchpoints; involve in key decisions; anticipate concerns before they escalate |
| **Keep satisfied** | High | Low | Regular briefings; don't surprise them; seek their input on decisions in their domain |
| **Keep informed** | Low | High | Status updates; include in working sessions when relevant; surface their input to high-power stakeholders |
| **Monitor** | Low | Low | Minimal contact; notify of decisions that affect them; don't invest relationship capital here |

## Stakeholder Interview Protocol

### Before the interview

- Review their background (LinkedIn, org chart, prior meeting notes)
- Define your objective: what decision, information, or support do you need from this person?
- Prepare 5-7 questions; expect to use only 3-4

### Interview structure (60-90 min)

| Phase | Duration | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Opening** | 5 min | Build rapport; explain purpose of the conversation |
| **Situation exploration** | 20 min | Understand their view of the problem from their vantage point |
| **Priorities and concerns** | 20 min | Understand what success looks like from their perspective |
| **History** | 10 min | What has been tried before? Why did it not work? |
| **Closing** | 10 min | Who else should we talk to? What concerns do they have about the engagement? |

### Questions for each stakeholder type

**For sponsors (high power):**
- "If this engagement is successful, what will have changed in 18 months?"
- "What is the one thing you are most worried could derail this?"
- "What decisions are you prepared to make based on our recommendations?"

**For process owners / implementers (high interest, lower power):**
- "What do you think is really driving this problem?"
- "What constraints should we be aware of that people above you might not mention?"
- "If you had the authority, what would you change first?"

**For skeptics:**
- "What concerns do you have about this engagement?"
- "What would we have to show you for you to be confident in the recommendations?"
- "Have you seen similar efforts fail before? What happened?"

### Interview output

Document each interview with:
1. Stakeholder name, title, date
2. Key perspectives on the problem
3. Stated priorities and concerns
4. Position on proposed change (champion/neutral/skeptic + rationale)
5. Names of additional people to interview

## Engaging Skeptics and Blockers

Skeptics are not adversaries — they are often the most informed people about why previous efforts failed.

**Skeptic engagement protocol:**

1. **Meet them early** — a skeptic who feels heard is less likely to mobilize opposition
2. **Name their concern explicitly** — "I understand there's a concern that [X]. I'd like to understand it better."
3. **Seek their input, not their approval** — ask "what would we need to show you?" not "can you support this?"
4. **Acknowledge legitimate objections** — if their concern is valid, say so; credibility is more valuable than winning the conversation
5. **Document their positions** — report to the sponsor which stakeholders are skeptical and why; sponsors need this information

**Red flags with skeptics:**
- A high-power skeptic who refuses to meet with the consulting team is a project risk — escalate to the sponsor immediately
- A skeptic who seems to have an ulterior motive (e.g., the engagement threatens their function) needs to be understood as a political stakeholder, not just a business stakeholder

## Common Mistakes in Stakeholder Management

| Mistake | Consequence | Prevention |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| Mapping only champions | Blindsided by blockers at presentation | Actively identify skeptics in first week |
| Treating stakeholder map as static | Missing shifts in position mid-engagement | Update after every major meeting |
| Over-investing in low-power stakeholders | Wasted relationship capital; missed high-power stakeholders | Quadrant matrix is a resource allocation tool |
| Conflating "informed" with "engaged" | Stakeholders say "I wasn't consulted" even after email updates | Distinguish broadcast communication from genuine involvement |
| Not telling the sponsor about the blockers | Sponsor is surprised at steering committee | Monthly stakeholder sentiment summary to sponsor |
